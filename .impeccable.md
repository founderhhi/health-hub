## Design Context

### Users
Health Hub is a consumer-facing healthcare application for patients and their families who need quick, trustworthy access to care. People use it to understand symptoms, request a GP, receive referrals, join consultations, manage prescriptions, and review treatment information without feeling overwhelmed or lost.

Primary use cases:
- Start care quickly from a phone with minimal friction.
- Move confidently through GP, specialist, pharmacy, and records flows.
- Understand what is happening now, what happens next, and what action is available.
- Feel safe enough to trust the product during moments of stress or uncertainty.

### Brand Personality
Trustworthy, warm, and quietly premium.

The interface should feel:
- Calm rather than clinical-cold.
- Clear and guided rather than dense or admin-heavy.
- Modern and polished rather than generic or templated.

### Aesthetic Direction
Design for a mobile-first consumer health experience with a refined, human tone. Use the existing emerald-led brand as the base, but push it toward a more intentional and memorable identity through stronger hierarchy, better empty/loading states, better spacing rhythm, and more expressive typography where appropriate.

Preferred direction:
- Light-first or balanced theming that feels welcoming and legible.
- Soft medical credibility with a consumer-product finish.
- Layered surfaces, purposeful accents, and clearer information grouping.

Avoid:
- Generic AI-dashboard aesthetics.
- Purple/cyan gradients, glassmorphism, and card-inside-card layouts.
- Over-explaining copy and repetitive headings.
- Frontend that feels unfinished, placeholder-driven, or operational instead of patient-ready.

### Design Principles
1. Make care pathways feel understandable at a glance: every screen should show current status, next step, and primary action clearly.
2. Design for emotional reassurance: reduce stress with calm copy, visible progress, and stable visual hierarchy.
3. Prioritize mobile clarity over feature density: fewer, stronger visual decisions beat crowded dashboards.
4. Favor distinctive but credible presentation: polished, consumer-friendly, and memorable without becoming flashy.
5. Treat loading, empty, error, and coming-soon states as first-class product surfaces, not afterthoughts.
